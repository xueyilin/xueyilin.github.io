join
Creating a Purchase Share Platform

Role: Product Designer | Researcher | UX/UI Designer

Individual Project      Date: 2016/9-2016/12

Category: Interaction DesignProduct Design Interface Design

Intro

As part of my Introduction to Interaction Design course, I was required to design an interactive system for a community that I am a part of. I want to find a community that I can easily access to, so I've decided to scope it down to students on campus.

This project is inspired by a common marketing technique known as buy more save more. Lots of website offers this type of deal, where the more you buy the more discount you get.

Seeing that the University community is a group that are:

  • Geographically close to each other

  • In strong need of savings.

  • Have excessive wants to purchase

I wonder if thereâ€™s a way to use this type of deal to benefit the student body on-campus who are tight on budget by helping them save more without buying more.

Goal

Help University of Michigan students leverage the large on-campus peer community to purchase with better discounts.

Approach

During the process, I want to try and incorporate User-Centered Design and Participatory Design, so that in all stages and for all decisions, the end users are involved. Thus, every iteration of design was tested with users.

Research

Competitive Analysis: Identify Opportunities

The first thing that I did was research on products in market that serves similar purposes. Although Groupon and Amazon Prime both offers a certain type of discount for students, it is still not as flexible as purchasing directly from brand websites.

Interviews: Understand User Context

To better scope the problem and point it to actual user needs, I conducted three preliminary interviews. This includes:

  • Someone who in the past has facilitated group purchase activity with others

  • Someone who has participated in group purchase with others

  • Someone who has never participated in this type of activity but is an excessive shopper

Through interviews, I discovered that although some students have tried to achieve this type of sales by purchasing with friends and merging their orders, but there are several problem with the current approach:

  • Most of the time, they can't find enough people to reach the minimum amount of a deal.

  • When they manage to find enough people, they have to endure long wait-time for other participants to decide.

  • After the deal succeed, participants also face pressure of paying for others and being repaid

Building Personas: Show Empathy

With the interviews and the observation in chat groups that are facilitating this type of activity, I drafted out 3 personas and 1 anti-persona, to use as reference for future design.

Ideation

After coming up with the personas, I started ideating solutions that fit the identified user needs. The solution that I'm trying to design, aims to solve for these needs:

  • Find participants that can purchase together easily and efficiently

  • Have a mechanism that avoids the long wait time and people bailing out last minute that often happens while purchasing together

  • Avoid the hassle in encountering monetary dispute while buying together

Brainstorming via Sketch

With that in mind, I started brainstorming possible solutions that aims to either solve all or certain parts of the problem. The goal is to explore as many options as possible. Then I moved on to creating story board for each solution thinking how the people with the personas will use it and testing the idea with people I know.

Eliminate App as an Option to Fit User Context

After the first round of brainstorm, I conducted second round of interview with my previous interviewees, wanting to know if anything among these solutions stand out for them.

Through interviews, I find out that University student's shopping behavior is a lot different from what I imagined. While I was making the sketches, I assumed that a lot of shopping goes through people's mobile phone, and that's where I dedicated most of my attention to. In reality, students in University mostly shop through their laptop.

"I mostly purchase things with my laptop, because as a student, laptop is always with me"
"There's not a legit mobile app for most of the brand websites I shop at, and filling out payment information with my phone is just painful."

After developing better understanding of user's habit for shopping, I decided to focus on making a website instead of an app.

Solution Overview

My designed solution is a website called join.com, that helps students who want to do online purchase and need some extra saving find peers who are also interested with similar products or brand to connect, and merge their purchase to achieve better discount. The platform will allow students to submit deals they find or join other deals available without the social pressure of being rejected.

Exploration

After the initial brainstorming, I've started exploring options for designing one of the most important feature of the system: joining a deal.

Option 1: Dropping a link and submit

Option 1 emphasize on efficiency and privacy, but it lack a sense of community and flexibility because the user can't negotiate on each other, and all purchase are solely reliant on the platform to facilitate.

Option 2: Joining a chat to settle the deal

This approach advocates flexibility and simplicity because it is very similar to the existing way of participating in a co-purchase activity. However, it lacks efficiency and privacy, since users will still have to wait for others to make decision and reveal what they want to buy to the person who is paying.

Option 3: Submit a request and automatically merge purchases

Its simple and efficient, but the mechanism is also not very flexible. Most importantly, having users making requests first can result in users not knowing what is available or submitting very niche requests that no one is responding.

To help in deciding which option better solves the user's problem, I conducted a QOC analysis to assess each options strength and shortcoming, with five criterias: efficiency, privacy, simplicity, social, and flexibility.

After assessing all the strengths and trade offs, I've decided to use the first approach, which is dropping a link to join. It resonates the best with the key pain points located through my user research, which is the current process being overly timely and complicated.

Testing: Form to Step-by-Step Submission

One thing that I've realized while testing paper prototype is that users find it hard to understand the purpose of this website just by interacting with it.

My initial approach was to design more pages so that the user can see through the whole process. However, after I've tested a low-fi digital version made with InVision in class, I realized the key problem of this is the unclear instruction in submitting a deal.

Low-fi Digital Prototype for Deal Submission

The low-fi version was originally designed as a all-in-one form that user can fill out and submit. My original thought was that this way, users can quickly and easily submit a deal.

However, due to the complex context behind submitting a deal, and the two dynamics that I've predefined:

  • A site deal such as getting 20% off from orders over $100 that different users can join with a list of products they want to purchase from that site.

  • A product such as getting cheaper price buying in bulk where users can decide to take a share of the bulk under cheaper unit price.

Users have a hard time understanding the difference and what link to submit while interacting with the prototype.

"I'm not sure what to input for all the columns"
"I don't understand the difference between site deal and product deal"
"It is really frustrating to fill out so much all at once"

This made me transform the original deal submission form to a step-by-step process, where it guides the user through the submission, and explains what various terms mean on each page.
